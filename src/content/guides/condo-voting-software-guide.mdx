Choosing **condo voting software** is a governance decision, not a generic IT purchase. The platform you select will shape how owners experience director elections, quorum, proxies, and ballots — and whether results withstand scrutiny if someone challenges the outcome.

This guide helps Ontario boards, managers, and committees evaluate **voting platforms** systematically. For the broader case for e-voting, start with [electronic voting for Ontario condos](/guides/electronic-voting-ontario-condos). For a method comparison, see [proxy voting vs electronic voting](/guides/proxy-voting-vs-electronic-voting).

## What condo voting software should do

General meeting tools — video conferencing, survey apps, shared drives — are not the same as **purpose-built condo voting software**. At minimum, a suitable platform should support:

### Voter list management

- Import units and owners from corporation records
- Flag eligibility issues before voting opens
- Prevent duplicate votes per unit across channels

### Ballot configuration

- Director elections with multiple open seats
- Yes/no or threshold votes for bylaw and special resolutions
- Configurable **secret ballots** for contested races

### Proxy integration

- Digital proxy assignment or tracking where your process requires it
- Clear rules when an owner both proxies and attempts to vote electronically

### Quorum and participation reporting

- Live counts of units represented
- Dashboards for the chair and scrutineers without exposing vote choices

### Scrutineer and audit tools

- Role-based access for independent observers
- Exportable **results and participation logs** for minutes and retention

### Owner experience

- Plain-language instructions and accessible support
- Mobile-friendly interfaces — many owners vote from phones

### Live meeting support

- Technical help during the AGM when login spikes or questions arise

Platforms oriented to Ontario condos — including **ElectoSense** — typically bundle these functions because paper proxies and hybrid meetings remain common.

## Evaluation criteria

Use the table below as a scorecard when reviewing vendors.

| Category | Questions to ask |
| --- | --- |
| **Ontario fit** | How many Ontario condos use the platform? Do you understand statutory proxies and AGM timing? |
| **Security** | Encryption, access controls, hosting location, penetration testing |
| **Privacy** | PIPEDA-aligned practices; who can see owner data and vote records |
| **Reliability** | Uptime history; redundancy during peak voting windows |
| **Integrations** | Virtual meeting links, property management exports, PDF minutes |
| **Support** | Dedicated AGM-day support vs email-only tickets |
| **Pricing** | Per-unit, per-meeting, or annual fees; hidden setup costs |
| **References** | Similar-sized corporations willing to share experience |

Document answers. Boards change; records of why you chose a vendor protect future directors.

## Security and ballot integrity

Owners will ask: **Is my vote secret? Can management see how I voted?**

A credible vendor separates:

- **Authentication** — proving the voter is the eligible owner
- **Ballot secrecy** — restricting access to choices after submission
- **Audit data** — proving participation without revealing content

Ask for a written description of the **vote lifecycle** — creation, delivery, submission, tally, and export. Avoid vendors who cannot explain this clearly.

## Implementation timeline

Rushing software live the week before an AGM is a common failure mode. A realistic timeline:

1. **Weeks 1–2:** Board decision, legal review of bylaws, vendor shortlist
2. **Weeks 3–4:** Contract, voter list preparation, notice language drafting
3. **Weeks 5–6:** Owner communication, test credentials, scrutineer training
4. **Meeting week:** Voting opens per notice; support on AGM day; results export to minutes

If bylaws must be amended to authorize electronic voting, add time for **owner notice and vote** on the amendment itself. Our [board elections guide](/guides/ontario-condo-board-elections) covers election notice requirements.

## Common mistakes

### Skipping bylaw review

Software cannot override governing documents. Confirm authorization before collecting digital votes.

### Poor owner communication

Sending a login link without explanation invites low uptake and suspicion. Explain **why** the corporation is changing process and **how** to get help.

### No scrutineer plan

Independent scrutineers still matter. Ensure they receive platform access and training before the meeting.

### Ignoring hybrid scenarios

Plan how in-person, proxy, and electronic votes reconcile. Define which channel wins if an owner uses more than one.

### Choosing on price alone

The cheapest tool that lacks AGM support may cost more in **adjourned meetings** and manual fallbacks.

## Who should be involved

| Role | Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| **Board** | Approve method, budget, and owner communication tone |
| **Manager** | Voter list accuracy, logistics, vendor coordination |
| **Legal counsel** | Bylaw and notice review when needed |
| **Scrutineers** | Independent oversight of tally and challenges |
| **Vendor** | Platform configuration, support, exports |

Owners benefit when the corporation publishes a **single FAQ** linking to [Ontario condo voting rights](/guides/ontario-condo-voting-rights) and plain instructions.

## After the meeting

Good software still requires **post-meeting discipline**:

- Export and store results with minutes
- Archive participation records per retention practices
- Debrief — what worked, what confused owners, what to fix next cycle

If results are challenged, exported logs support transparent review without reopening individual ballots improperly.
