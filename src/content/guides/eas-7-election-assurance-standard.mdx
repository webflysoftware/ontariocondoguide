Most Ontario condo election problems are not discovered on election night. They surface **weeks later** — when an owner asks who was eligible, whether a proxy was counted twice, or how the chair arrived at the final tally. By then, paper stacks have been filed, memories have faded, and the corporation is arguing about process instead of outcomes.

**EAS-7 (Election Assurance Standard)** is a seven-part readiness framework for evaluating whether an election process is **private, auditable, traceable, and defensible** before a single ballot is cast. It is not a law, a certification, or a vendor checklist. It is a governance lens: seven control areas that boards, managers, scrutineers, and inspectors can walk through together while there is still time to fix gaps.

Use EAS-7 alongside your [condo election checklist](/templates/condo-election-checklist), the [election readiness check](/tools/election-readiness-check), and — when electronic voting is involved — the [digital voting readiness checklist](/templates/digital-voting-readiness-checklist).

## What EAS-7 means

Election assurance is the degree of confidence that:

- Only **eligible voters** participated
- **Ballot secrecy** was preserved where required
- Each voter cast **at most one valid ballot**
- The process can be **reconstructed** after the meeting
- **Turnout** was visible without exposing choices
- **Proxies and special submissions** were controlled and reviewable
- **Results** can be explained to the board, manager, scrutineer, and owners

EAS-7 names seven control areas that must hold for that confidence to be reasonable. Weakness in any one area can invalidate trust in the whole election — even if the vote count arithmetic is correct.

## When to use EAS-7

Run an EAS-7 review **before notice goes out** for any election where:

- Director seats are contested or factions are active
- The corporation is adopting electronic or hybrid voting for the first time
- A prior meeting failed quorum or was challenged
- Proxy collection is expected to be heavy
- Owners have raised concerns about fairness, access, or counting

EAS-7 is equally useful for **paper-only** elections. Paper processes often fail silently on duplicate prevention and audit reconstruction — the areas where disputes become hardest to resolve.

For broader election planning context, see [how condo board elections work](/guides/how-condo-board-elections-work) and the [Ontario condo board election guide](/guides/ontario-condo-board-elections).

## Who should be involved

| Role | EAS-7 responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| **Board / governance committee** | Authorizes the process, confirms bylaws and notice align with controls |
| **Property manager** | Executes voter list, logistics, owner communication, record retention |
| **Chair** | Ensures meeting procedure matches the pre-approved control design |
| **Scrutineer(s) / inspector** | Validates eligibility, challenges, and tally integrity at the meeting |
| **Legal counsel** | Advises on statutory and document requirements where outcomes are high-stakes |
| **Voting platform operator** | Implements technical controls when electronic voting is used |

EAS-7 works best when the scrutineer and chair review all seven areas **in writing** before voting opens — not improvised at the registration table.

## The seven control areas

### 1. Eligibility Assurance

**Control objective:** Only eligible voters, units, owners, or members can access the ballot.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Voter list reconciled with corporation records (ownership changes, liens, multiple owners per unit)
- Rules for co-owners, corporations-as-owners, and power-of-attorney holders documented
- Registration process rejects ineligible participants with a clear appeal path
- Electronic systems authenticate the voter against the list — not merely a shared login link

**Common failures:** Stale owner lists; units counted when fees are in arrears without a documented policy; guest or tenant ballots accepted in error.

**Evidence to retain:** Final voter list snapshot, eligibility decision log, registration records.

---

### 2. Ballot Secrecy

**Control objective:** Participation can be verified without exposing vote choices.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Director elections use a **secret ballot** process consistent with your documents
- Scrutineers can confirm *that* a unit voted without seeing *how* it voted
- Electronic systems **separate identity from ballot content** in storage and reporting
- No process allows the chair, manager, or vendor to browse individual choices after the vote

**Common failures:** Email votes with identifiable senders; proxy holders announcing votes aloud; dashboards that display choices per unit.

**Evidence to retain:** Written description of secrecy architecture (paper or digital), scrutineer attestation.

---

### 3. Duplicate Prevention

**Control objective:** Each eligible voter can submit only one valid ballot.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Rules defined when an owner holds a proxy **and** attempts to vote personally
- Electronic systems enforce one ballot per unit across channels (advance, live, in-person)
- Paper ballot serial numbers or equivalent tracking prevent double issuance
- Revoked proxies removed from the count before polls close

**Common failures:** Owner votes electronically and proxy still counted; duplicate paper proxies from the same unit; advance votes not reconciled at registration.

**Evidence to retain:** Duplicate-rejection log, revoked-proxy register, channel reconciliation worksheet.

---

### 4. Audit Trail

**Control objective:** Election setup, voter access, reminders, changes, and results can be reconstructed afterward.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Configuration changes (ballot text, open seats, voting window) are logged with timestamp and author
- Owner access events (login, ballot submission, proxy assignment) are recorded
- Reminder and notice sends are traceable
- Results export is **immutable** or hash-verified after close — not editable spreadsheets

**Common failures:** Results retyped into Excel with no source file; no record of who changed ballot options mid-process; paper proxies with no custody chain.

**Evidence to retain:** Full audit log export, configuration snapshot at poll open, results file with checksum or signed export.

---

### 5. Turnout Visibility

**Control objective:** Participation can be tracked without compromising ballot secrecy.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Quorum calculation method documented (units represented, not votes cast per motion)
- Live turnout dashboard available to chair and scrutineer **without** vote-choice data
- Advance and electronic participation reconciled with in-person registration before quorum is declared
- Owners understand turnout is visible; secrecy applies to choices, not to the fact of participation

**Common failures:** Quorum declared from an approximate head count; no visibility until after the meeting; turnout reports that leak preferences.

**Evidence to retain:** Turnout snapshots at key times, quorum declaration worksheet, [quorum tracking sheet](/templates/quorum-tracking-sheet).

Use the [quorum calculator](/tools/quorum-calculator) during planning.

---

### 6. Proxy & Submission Control

**Control objective:** Proxies, nominations, write-ins, and special submissions are recorded and reviewable.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Proxy forms meet statutory and document requirements; invalid forms rejected with documented reason
- Nomination period dates and candidate eligibility rules published
- Write-in and special ballot handling defined before the meeting — not decided ad hoc
- Custody of proxies from collection through scrutineer handoff is documented

**Common failures:** Competing proxy forms with no rejection standard; nominations accepted after deadline; write-ins counted without a defined procedure.

**Evidence to retain:** Proxy register, nomination log, rejected-form log with reasons, [proxy checklist](/templates/proxy-checklist) completion record.

For proxy risk context, see [proxy warfare in Ontario condo voting](/guides/proxy-warfare-ontario-condo-voting).

---

### 7. Result Defensibility

**Control objective:** The final tally can be explained to the board, manager, scrutineer, and voters.

**What to verify before voting opens:**

- Tabulation method documented (manual count, electronic export, hybrid reconciliation)
- Scrutineer sign-off process defined before results are announced
- Challenged ballots handled through a published rule, not chair discretion alone
- Results package includes enough detail for minutes and post-meeting review without reopening secrecy

**Common failures:** Results announced before scrutineer review; no written reconciliation between channels; inability to answer "how many units voted for each candidate" without contradicting earlier statements.

**Evidence to retain:** Signed [voting results record](/templates/voting-results-record), scrutineer report, exported tally with audit cross-reference.

## EAS-7 readiness summary

| # | Control area | Pass / gap | Owner | Target date |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Eligibility Assurance | | | |
| 2 | Ballot Secrecy | | | |
| 3 | Duplicate Prevention | | | |
| 4 | Audit Trail | | | |
| 5 | Turnout Visibility | | | |
| 6 | Proxy & Submission Control | | | |
| 7 | Result Defensibility | | | |

A **pass** means the control is designed, documented, assigned, and testable — not merely intended. A **gap** requires remediation or a documented risk acceptance by the board before voting proceeds.

Do not treat partial compliance as assurance. One broken control — especially duplicate prevention or ballot secrecy — can undermine every other.

## Common mistakes when applying EAS-7

- **Reviewing controls after the meeting** instead of before notice
- **Assigning assurance to the vendor alone** while the board retains statutory responsibility
- **Confusing turnout with consent** — high participation does not fix invalid process
- **Skipping written evidence** and relying on verbal confirmation from the chair
- **Treating electronic voting as automatic assurance** without mapping each EAS-7 control to platform behaviour

## Pre-vote checklist

Before opening the ballot, confirm:

- [ ] All seven EAS-7 control areas reviewed and documented
- [ ] Scrutineer has signed off on control design (not just meeting attendance)
- [ ] Voter list frozen with eligibility rules applied
- [ ] Ballot secrecy and duplicate-prevention rules tested in a dry run
- [ ] Audit logging and results export tested end-to-end
- [ ] Turnout and quorum tracking method agreed with the chair
- [ ] Proxy and nomination registers complete and reconciled
- [ ] Results tabulation and sign-off procedure written and shared
- [ ] Evidence retention plan defined (what is stored, where, for how long)

## Closing note

Election assurance is not paranoia. It is **basic infrastructure** for any corporation where owners trust their home, fees, and governance to the outcome of a vote. EAS-7 gives boards and scrutineers a shared vocabulary to ask the right questions early — when fixes are still possible.

This guide provides general educational information, not legal advice. Confirm requirements under the Condominium Act, your governing documents, and qualified counsel for your corporation's specific election.
