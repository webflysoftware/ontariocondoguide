Quorum is one of those governance terms that sounds technical until a meeting actually starts — and then everyone in the room realizes how much depends on it. Without quorum, an Ontario condominium corporation generally cannot elect directors, approve budgets, pass bylaw amendments, or take other binding votes. The meeting may still open, but the business owners came for is on hold.

Understanding quorum is not just a board responsibility. Owners who want their vote to matter — and corporations that want to avoid costly adjournments — need a clear picture of how participation is counted and what to do when turnout is uncertain.

## What quorum means in an Ontario condo

In condominium governance, **quorum** is the minimum level of owner representation required before a meeting can proceed with business. It is a safeguard: decisions that bind every owner should not be made when almost nobody is participating.

Under Ontario's Condominium Act, 1998, quorum requirements for owners' meetings are typically found in the Act itself and in your corporation's **declaration** and **bylaws**. For many corporations, the default threshold is **25% of the voting units** represented at the meeting. Some declarations specify a higher percentage or different rules for particular types of meetings.

"Represented" usually means the owner is present in person, participating through a permitted remote method, or counted through a **valid proxy**. Each voting unit typically counts once, regardless of how many people own it or how many proxies they hold for other units.

## Who counts — and who does not

Getting quorum right starts with knowing what a **voting unit** is in your corporation. It is usually each unit as defined in the declaration, though parking or locker units may or may not carry separate votes depending on your documents.

Common counting rules:

- **One unit, one vote** for quorum in standard owners' meetings.
- **Proxies** count the unit they represent if the form is valid and submitted on time.
- **Co-owners** of a single unit do not double-count; one unit still equals one vote.
- **Mortgagees or tenants** generally do not vote unless the declaration grants them that right — which is uncommon for routine owners' meetings.

The chair or meeting administrator should maintain a **registration record** that ties each person or proxy to a specific unit. That record becomes essential if someone challenges whether quorum was ever achieved.

## How to calculate quorum for your corporation

The math is straightforward once you know your total voting units and the percentage required:

1. Confirm the total number of voting units in the corporation (from the declaration or unit register).
2. Identify the quorum percentage in your declaration, bylaws, or the Act.
3. Multiply total units by that percentage and round according to your governing documents or standard practice.
4. Track represented units through registration and proxy logs until the threshold is met.

For a 200-unit building with a 25% quorum rule, you need **50 units represented**. If 30 owners attend in person and 22 valid proxies are held, you have 52 units — quorum is met, provided no unit is counted twice.

## Proxies and quorum: the practical link

In many Ontario condos, **proxies drive quorum** more than in-person attendance. That is especially true in larger buildings, seasonal communities, and corporations with many investor-owners who rarely attend.

A proxy does not automatically fix every participation problem. The form must comply with the Condominium Act and your corporation's requirements — including timing, signatures, and scope of authority. Invalid or late proxies cannot be counted, which is why boards often discover at the meeting door that quorum is still out of reach.

Owners who cannot attend should understand how to complete a proxy correctly. See the [Ontario condo proxy form guide](/guides/ontario-condo-proxy-form) and the [proxy checklist](/templates/proxy-checklist) for a step-by-step review before submitting.

## When quorum is in doubt: planning ahead

Experienced managers and boards treat quorum as a **planning metric**, not a surprise on meeting night. Useful steps include:

- **Early notice** with clear instructions on attendance and proxy submission.
- **Proxy collection windows** with cut-off dates that allow verification before the meeting.
- **Reminders** to owners who have not responded as the meeting approaches.
- **Hybrid or electronic participation** where permitted, so remote owners count without travelling.
- **A quorum tracking process** from the moment registration opens.

If your corporation chronically struggles with turnout, the issue may be structural — timing, communication, proxy complexity, or owner distrust — not just apathy. Addressing those factors early reduces the risk of adjournment.

## Quorum versus voting majorities

A common source of confusion is the difference between **quorum** and **voting thresholds**. Quorum is about whether there are enough units represented to conduct business at all. Voting thresholds — such as a majority of those present and voting, or a higher percentage for bylaw amendments — determine whether a specific motion passes once quorum exists.

You can have quorum and still lose a vote. You can also have a passionate debate with many speakers and still lack quorum if too few units are formally represented. Keep both concepts separate when explaining meeting rules to owners.

## What happens if quorum is not met

If the corporation cannot establish quorum within the time allowed at the meeting, the chair typically adjourns. Depending on your documents and the type of meeting, the corporation may reschedule, extend the meeting to another date, or follow specific adjournment rules.

Repeated quorum failures delay director elections, budget approvals, and other essential business. They also erode owner confidence. For a detailed walkthrough of adjournment, reconvened meetings, and owner options, read [what happens if quorum is not met](/guides/what-happens-if-quorum-not-met).

## Chair responsibilities at the meeting

The chair plays a central role in quorum management:

- Open registration early and keep it accurate throughout.
- Confirm when quorum is achieved and announce it to owners.
- Do not put substantive motions to a vote until quorum is established.
- If quorum is lost during the meeting — for example, if enough represented owners leave — address the situation according to applicable rules and seek legal advice if the scenario is unclear.

Document the quorum count in the **minutes**, including the number of units represented and the method used to verify attendance.

## Takeaways for boards and owners

Quorum is the gateway to legitimate condo decision-making. Boards should communicate requirements clearly, verify proxies before meeting night, and use tracking tools rather than estimates. Owners should treat participation — in person or by proxy — as part of protecting their investment.

When in doubt about the rule that applies to your corporation, review your declaration and bylaws and consult a condominium lawyer. The general principles in this guide are educational; your governing documents control.
