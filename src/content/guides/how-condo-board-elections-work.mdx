Board elections look simple on the agenda — "election of directors" — but the mechanics underneath involve nominations, eligibility, ballots, proxies, and a tally that everyone present can trust. This guide explains how those pieces fit together at a typical Ontario condominium corporation.

For planning context and timing, start with our [Ontario condo board elections](/guides/ontario-condo-board-elections) overview. This article focuses on **what happens during the election itself**.

## Before the vote: nominations and eligibility

An election starts long before anyone marks a ballot. The corporation must know **how many seats** are open, who is eligible to stand, and who is eligible to vote.

### Director qualifications

The Condominium Act sets **qualification and disqualification** rules for directors. Common issues include conflicts of interest, arrears of common expenses in some circumstances, and age or capacity requirements. Your bylaws may add further rules. Each nominee should confirm eligibility in writing before being listed on the ballot or acknowledged from the floor.

### Nomination methods

Corporations typically use one or more of:

- **Written nominations** submitted before the meeting deadline in the notice
- **Nominations from the floor** at the meeting, if permitted
- **Acclamation** when the number of nominees equals or is less than open seats

If more candidates run than there are seats, the election is **contested** and a ballot vote is usually required. If fewer candidates than seats come forward, those nominated may be elected by acclamation, and the corporation must address any remaining vacancies under its documents.

## Opening the election at the meeting

Once the chair confirms **quorum** and deals with preliminary agenda items, the election segment generally follows this pattern:

1. **Announce open seats** and confirm the list of candidates
2. **Appoint scrutineer(s)** and explain how ballot challenges will be handled
3. **Distribute ballots** or open voting by the approved method
4. **Collect votes** — in person, by proxy, by written ballot, or electronically if authorized
5. **Count and announce results**
6. **Record results in the minutes**

If quorum is lost mid-meeting or the election is out of order due to a procedural defect, the chair may need to adjourn. See our [quorum guide](/guides/ontario-condo-quorum) for how participation thresholds work.

## Voting methods in detail

### Show of hands

Used mainly when candidates are unopposed. It is quick but offers little privacy and a weak audit trail. Many corporations prefer ballots once any contest exists.

### Written ballots

Each eligible voter receives a ballot listing candidates (or space for write-ins if allowed). Voters mark their choices privately. Ballots are collected in a sealed container or counted at the table with scrutineers observing.

Best practices:

- Use **pre-printed ballots** with correct seat numbers
- Mark units or proxies on an envelope or register without exposing vote choices
- Do not open or discard challenged ballots without following the announced process

### Proxy voting

An owner who cannot attend may appoint a **proxy** to vote on their behalf using the statutory proxy form and any additional requirements in your documents. The proxyholder attends (or submits the proxy, depending on the method) and votes according to the owner's instructions — or exercises discretion if the form allows.

Proxy voting is common in Ontario but creates logistical complexity. Competing proxy campaigns, duplicate forms, and last-minute revocations are frequent sources of dispute. Owners should understand their rights under [Ontario condo voting rights](/guides/ontario-condo-voting-rights).

### Electronic voting

Where properly authorized, owners may vote electronically before or during the meeting. Electronic methods can improve access for absent owners and create an audit trail — but require upfront bylaw review, notice, identity verification, and vendor or platform selection. See [electronic voting for Ontario condos](/guides/electronic-voting-ontario-condos) for when and how corporations consider this option.

## How votes are counted

For a standard plurality election with multiple open seats, each eligible voter may vote for **up to the number of vacancies**. Candidates receiving the most votes fill the seats in order.

Example: three open seats, five candidates — each voter may select up to three names. After counting, the three candidates with the highest vote totals are elected.

Some corporations use variations (for example, ranked or cumulative voting) if their bylaws expressly provide for them. **Default rules apply** unless your documents say otherwise.

Scrutineers verify:

- The number of ballots matches eligible voters or proxies accepted
- Spoiled or ambiguous ballots are set aside under the announced rules
- The arithmetic of the tally before the chair declares results

## After the vote

The chair **declares the results** aloud and records them in the **minutes**. Newly elected directors may need to sign declarations, disclose conflicts, and receive handover materials from outgoing directors.

If an owner believes the election was flawed — for example, due to defective notice or improper proxy handling — they should document concerns promptly and seek advice on remedies available under the Act. Prevention remains the best strategy: use the [condo election checklist](/templates/condo-election-checklist) and run the [election readiness check](/tools/election-readiness-check) before notice goes out.

## Making elections understandable for owners

Many owners attend their first AGM unsure how voting works. A one-page summary in the notice package — how many seats, who is nominated, how to vote in person or by proxy, and whether electronic voting is available — reduces confusion and supports fairer participation.

Clear mechanics will not eliminate every governance conflict. They do ensure that when disagreements arise, the corporation can point to a process that was visible, consistent, and worth defending.
