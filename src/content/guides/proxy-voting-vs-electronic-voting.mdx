Ontario condominium corporations have long relied on **paper proxies** so absent owners can participate in AGMs and elections. Electronic voting offers a different path — verified digital ballots, participation tracking, and audit records — but it requires authorization, planning, and owner trust.

Neither method is perfect. This guide compares **proxy voting** and **electronic voting** in plain language so boards and owners can choose — or combine — approaches that fit their corporation.

## What each method looks like

### Paper proxy voting

An owner completes a **statutory proxy form** appointing someone to attend and vote on their behalf. Proxies may be collected by mail, email scan, or in person before the meeting. The proxyholder (or the chair, if named) exercises votes according to instructions on the form.

### Electronic voting

An owner receives access to a **secure voting channel** — often by email verification — and casts a ballot directly without appointing an intermediary. Participation is logged; individual vote choices remain private when the system is designed for secret ballots.

Some corporations use **electronic proxy assignment** as part of a broader platform, blending digital convenience with proxy law where needed.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Factor | Paper proxies | Electronic voting |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Owner effort** | Find form, sign, deliver before deadline | Register once, vote online — often minutes |
| **Accessibility for absent owners** | Works if forms arrive on time | Works if owners receive credentials and support |
| **Privacy in contested elections** | Proxyholder knows how they vote; not a true secret ballot for the owner | Can offer direct secret ballot |
| **Audit trail** | Paper custody chains; gaps are common | Digital logs of participation and timestamps |
| **Quorum visibility** | Manual count at registration | Often real-time dashboards |
| **Dispute risk** | Duplicate forms, revocations, authenticity questions | System errors, access issues — but fewer custody disputes |
| **Setup cost** | Low upfront; high labour at scale | Platform fees; lower manual handling |
| **Bylaw requirements** | Long-established in most corporations | Must confirm authorization in governing documents |

## When paper proxies still make sense

Proxies remain appropriate when:

- Your corporation has **not yet authorized** electronic voting
- The meeting is **small**, turnout is manageable, and owners prefer familiar forms
- **Budget constraints** rule out a platform for a single uncontested AGM
- Owners have limited internet access and paper is the inclusive option

Even then, boards should use **clear deadlines**, **neutral scrutineers**, and **published rules** for challenged proxies. Owners should read [Ontario condo voting rights](/guides/ontario-condo-voting-rights) before signing anything.

## When electronic voting is worth considering

Electronic voting tends to pay off when:

- **Turnout is chronically low** and absent owners dominate paper proxies
- **Large unit counts** make manual proxy sorting slow and error-prone
- **Contested elections** need secret ballots and defensible tallies
- **Past disputes** centred on lost forms, duplicate proxies, or quorum miscounts
- **Hybrid or virtual AGMs** need voting aligned with remote attendance

## The hybrid model

Many Ontario corporations adopt **both**:

- **Electronic voting opens** several days before the meeting
- **Paper proxies and in-person votes** remain available for owners who prefer them
- **Scrutineers reconcile** so each unit votes once

Hybrids require careful rules. If an owner votes electronically and also submits a proxy, the corporation must define which vote counts. Good platforms **lock out duplicates** automatically; paper-only corporations need manual cross-checks.

## Participation and democracy

The best method is the one that produces **fair, verifiable outcomes** with **broad owner access**. Paper proxies often fail the verifiability test — not because every proxy is abused, but because **custody and authenticity are hard to prove** after a close election.

Electronic voting addresses several failure modes but introduces new ones: owners who never open the email, boards who skip owner communication, platforms chosen on price alone. Technology amplifies **good or bad process**; it does not replace governance judgment.

## Cost beyond the meeting

Compare:

- **Staff and manager hours** collecting and validating paper proxies
- **Re-noticed meetings** when quorum fails because proxies arrived too late
- **Legal fees** when election results are challenged
- **Platform fees** spread across one or two meetings per year

A corporation that spends thousands re-noticing AGMs may find electronic voting cheaper **on balance** — but only with proper bylaws, notice, and vendor selection. See [condo voting software guide](/guides/condo-voting-software-guide) for evaluation criteria.

## Recommendations by role

### For boards

- Do not change voting methods without **bylaw review** and owner notice
- Pilot credentials and instructions before the live AGM
- Appoint **scrutineers** who understand both electronic and paper paths in hybrid meetings

### For owners

- Treat proxies as **legal instruments** — read who you appoint and whether instructions are binding
- Ask whether **electronic voting** will be available before you give your proxy away
- Show up in person when you can; visibility keeps chairs honest

### For managers

- Build a **timeline** that includes voter list cleanup, platform setup, and proxy cutoffs
- Use the [condo election checklist](/templates/condo-election-checklist) for director elections

## Choosing a path forward

Proxy voting is not going away overnight, and electronic voting is not a magic fix. The direction for many Ontario corporations is **hybrid participation with stronger records** — less paper custody risk, more owners counted, and elections that survive scrutiny.
